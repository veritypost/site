Law · Constitutional

Supreme Court rules 6-3 that federal agencies cannot issue major regulations without explicit Congressional authorization

The decision overturns the 40-year-old Chevron doctrine and immediately affects pending EPA, SEC, and FTC rulemakings worth an estimated $2.1 trillion in economic impact.

The U.S. Supreme Court ruled 6-3 on Wednesday in Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo that federal agencies do not have inherent authority to interpret ambiguous statutes in their favor. Chief Justice Roberts, writing for the majority, held that courts must exercise independent judgment in determining the meaning of statutory provisions rather than deferring to agency interpretations. The ruling overturns Chevron U.S.A. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, the 1984 precedent that required courts to defer to reasonable agency interpretations of ambiguous laws. Justice Kagan, in dissent, wrote that the decision "guts Congress's ability to delegate technical decisions to expert agencies."
