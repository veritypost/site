Supreme Court overturns Chevron, ending 40 years of agency deference

The 6-3 ruling reshapes the balance between Congress, the courts, and every federal agency — and immediately threatens pending EPA, SEC, and FTC regulations worth $2.1 trillion.

Law · Constitutional · Lead story

The U.S. Supreme Court ruled 6-3 on Wednesday in Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo that federal agencies do not have inherent authority to interpret ambiguous statutes in their favor. Chief Justice Roberts, writing for the majority, held that courts must exercise independent judgment in determining the meaning of statutory provisions. The ruling overturns Chevron U.S.A. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, the 1984 precedent that required courts to defer to reasonable agency interpretations of ambiguous laws. Justice Kagan, in dissent, wrote that the decision "guts Congress's ability to delegate technical decisions to expert agencies."
