Supreme Court hears TikTok divestment challenge; ruling expected before January 19 deadline

The 2-hour, 40-minute argument concerned the April 2024 law requiring ByteDance to sell TikTok's U.S. operations or face a ban.

The U.S. Supreme Court on Friday heard oral argument in TikTok Inc. v. Garland, a challenge to the federal law requiring ByteDance to divest TikTok's United States operations or face prohibition. The argument ran 2 hours and 40 minutes, nine days before the law's effective date.

The law, the Protecting Americans from Foreign Adversary Controlled Applications Act, passed the House 352-65 last March and the Senate 79-18 in April. President Biden signed it the following day. The statute gives the executive branch authority to prohibit distribution of any mobile application controlled by a designated foreign adversary. ByteDance-owned TikTok is named explicitly in the text. TikTok has approximately 170 million users in the United States.

Solicitor General Elizabeth Prelogar argued for the government. Prelogar said the law addresses "the national security threat posed by the PRC's ability to demand access to data on 170 million Americans and to manipulate the content those Americans see." Noel Francisco argued for TikTok. Francisco said the law is "a speech restriction that Congress has never attempted before," and that it targets a single company by name without demonstrating a less-restrictive alternative. Both attorneys took questions from the justices for more than an hour each.

Chief Justice John Roberts asked Francisco whether Congress had acted on ByteDance's ownership structure rather than on the content of the platform. Justice Elena Kagan asked Prelogar whether the law would survive if Congress had named a U.S.-owned company instead. Justice Neil Gorsuch asked both attorneys about the scope of the government's national-security rationale. Justice Sonia Sotomayor asked Prelogar whether a divestment completed after the statutory deadline would cure the violation.

The D.C. Circuit Court of Appeals upheld the law unanimously last month, in a ruling by Chief Judge Sri Srinivasan joined by Judges Neomi Rao and Douglas Ginsburg. The Supreme Court granted certiorari and set expedited briefing soon after.

The Court did not indicate when it would issue a decision. The January 19 deadline is statutory; it does not adjust based on the Court's schedule. ByteDance has not publicly detailed a divestment plan that would satisfy the statute, and the government has not publicly stated what would qualify. No named First Amendment scholar at a peer-reviewed U.S. law school has publicly argued that the D.C. Circuit's reasoning was incorrect as of the time of Friday's argument; Francisco's contrary position is represented in the record through TikTok's brief and in Friday's oral argument.

The next scheduled event on the public docket is the Court's January 17 conference, at which the justices may act on any pending motion for a stay.
